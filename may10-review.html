Novelty fade is real (~30% week 2) Both pasta and chicken_2 dropped roughly a third in their second week — exactly what you'd expect from menu novelty. The honest steady-state for a new slot is ~70% of debut. Rotation cadence matters as much as slot count. Plan to rotate dishes within slots every 3–4 weeks to keep novelty pressure on.

Category-level cannibalization

The "Cheat" slot tells the story. In SD it spiked from 179 (Apr 26) → 252 (May 3, pasta debut) → 232 (May 10). The pasta launch pulled forward Cheat demand, then it normalized. Meanwhile the original "Chicken" slot dropped from 254 (Apr 12) → 143–153 (May 3 / May 10) — Chicken 2 ate roughly half its volume on debut, settled at ~30% cannibalization.

Hidden plot twist Cheat is now the #1 ordered slot in both cities — Cheat 212 DC / 252 SD on May 3, and Cheat 171 DC / 232 SD on May 10. Chicken used to be the top slot. The customer base is shifting toward comfort food. That's important context for what "premium" means going forward.

Premium Chef Special (week of May 10)

Surcharge launched in week 5/10. DC AOV (billed) jumped to $142, the highest in 7 weeks. SD AOV held at $145. Cash revenue records were set: SD $26.8k, DC $19.6k (just below the May 3 peak of $20.4k, despite Mother's Day skip drag).

Early read: premium chef is a real margin lever, not just substitution. Recommended: run a pricing A/B in week of May 17 to find the demand curve ($5 vs $8 surcharge).

AOV story — corrected (and the news is good)

An earlier version of this report used the Airtable Total Amount Billed formula, which recalculates dollar totals using today's prices. That made historical AOVs look ~$160–170 and produced a false "AOV declining" narrative.

Using the static Billing Amount field (actual cash charged), the real trajectory is the opposite:

  • DC AOV (billed): $133 → $135 → $127 → $131 → $135 → $135 → $142 (May 10 = highest)
  • SD AOV (billed): $147 → $143 → $139 → $142 → $139 → $146 → $145
  • DC $/order (all): $93 → $88 → $90 → $94 → $96 → $94 → $106 (+13% over 4 wks)
  • SD $/order (all): $98 → $94 → $99 → $97 → $99 → $108 → $108 (+11%)
The real picture Cart depth dipped (avg meals 5.97 → 5.33), but customers are paying more per order in cash. The premium-chef surcharge offset (and then some) the meal-count dip on May 10. Credit-usage is also dropping (no-bill share fell DC 30% → 26%; SD 33% → 25%) — meaning more orders are paying full price. Three positive forces stacking: more orders, more cash per order, less credit dependency. Stick with the menu expansion.
One thing still to watch Avg meals per order did drop from 6.0 → 5.3. Cash AOV held because per-item price is higher (and premium chef adds surcharge). But if cart depth keeps falling, eventually you stop being able to offset with pricing. Watch meal count, not just dollars.

07Adversarial deep-dive: what we missed

The first-pass takeaways were stress-tested. Here's where they bend or break.

AGREE — with caveats"Menu expansion is a hit"

Cash revenue is up sharply (DC +22%, SD +45% over 4 weeks). Cash AOV is up. New slots launched strong. But:

  • Week-2 attach decay ~30% — honest steady-state for a new slot is ~70% of debut. Plan a 3–4-week dish rotation cadence inside slots to keep novelty pressure on.
  • Some cannibalization is happening (chicken_2 took ~30% from the original chicken slot) — net positive overall, but it's not pure addition.
  • Avg meals per order dipped 6.0 → 5.3 — a real change, masked by higher pricing. If this keeps falling, eventually pricing won't fully offset.

AGREE"SD on track, DC needs intervention"

Sharper slope math: SD +16/wk (not +14), DC +8.5 including May 3 record / +7.4 excluding. The May 3 spike was triple-launch novelty stacking — treating it as the new normal is wishful thinking.

AGREE"Subscriber funnel is leaking"

Diagnosis sharpens: it's not "convert more subs" — it's "(a) raise new-cohort conversion rate" and "(b) stop adding low-intent zombies." DC has ~2,700 subscribers who joined >5 weeks ago and never ordered. Per the signup-conversion data, those leads are effectively dead. Segment comms by signup age:

  • Days 0–21: aggressive offer, weekly touch
  • Days 22–60: last-chance hail mary
  • Days 60+: once a month maximum, or remove from comms

DISAGREE (data correction)"AOV is declining"

Both my initial take AND the deep-dive critique were built on bad data — the Total Amount Billed formula field re-prices historical orders at today's pricing. Using Billing Amount (the actual static charged amount), cash AOV is up over 4 weeks (DC $131 → $142; SD $142 → $145) and per-order revenue including credit orders grew $94 → $106 in DC, $97 → $108 in SD. Cart depth did dip slightly (avg meals 5.97 → 5.33) but cash AOV held because per-item pricing went up + premium-chef surcharge added margin. Menu expansion is a cleaner win than I gave it credit for. Watch meal count going forward — that's the leading indicator.

AGREE — and under-weighted"1x-Lapsed is growing fast"

Single most important number. 1x → 2x conversion is 57.5% DC / 60% SD. Industry benchmark 65–75%. Closing the gap = +50 orders/wk steady-state. Top of the action plan.

PARTIAL"Mother's Day hit DC harder because of travelers"

The "DC travelers" story is unfalsifiable. The cleaner read: DC's active pool is structurally more fragile (per the segment-tail analysis). Mother's Day stress-tested it.

DISAGREE"Wellness Shots are modest"

Wrong framing. ~25% attach rate against returning customers is strong for a zero-menu-cost add-on. We should be testing bundles ($15 off 4 shots, or 3-in-3-weeks → next free) — not dismissing the channel.

What we missed entirely

  • The signup→first-order conversion window is just 5 weeks. By week 3, 76% of converters have already ordered; by week 5, 87%. Anyone past 5 weeks is mostly dead weight.
  • New-churn flow collapsed — DC 127 → 30/wk, SD 143 → 25/wk. We had a structurally good signal hiding in plain sight.
  • Cheat is now the #1 slot in both cities. Customer mindset is shifting toward comfort. The premium chef is launching into a base that wants pasta and braises, not lean protein.
  • Weekly-cadence is the real KPI — 70% DC / 72% SD of all returning orders are weekly. "Active customer count" is flattering vs "weekly-cadence customers."
  • Active segment instability — DC dropped 358 → 295 in a single week between snapshots (May 10 → May 11). The definition is recency-window sensitive. Treat the headline "Active" count as fuzzy.
  • Skip rate creep — bouncing around 28–36%. We haven't moved this in 6 weeks despite better comms.
